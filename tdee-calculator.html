How TDEE is calculated

TDEE is calculated in two steps using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, published in the Journal of the American Dietetic Association in 1990. It is the most validated predictive formula for resting energy expenditure in the general adult population.

Step 1: Calculate BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)

Men: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Step 2: Multiply by activity factor

Sedentary: BMR × 1.2
Lightly active (1–3 days/week): BMR × 1.375
Moderately active (3–5 days/week): BMR × 1.55
Very active (6–7 days/week): BMR × 1.725
Extra active (physical job or 2× daily): BMR × 1.9

The result is your TDEE — the total calories your body needs to maintain your current weight at your current activity level.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is TDEE?
    TDEE stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure. It is the total number of calories your body burns in a day — including resting metabolism plus calories burned through movement and exercise. It is the number you compare your food intake against to determine if you will lose, maintain, or gain weight.
  • What is a good TDEE?
    There is no single "good" TDEE — it depends on your body size, age, and activity level. A sedentary adult woman typically has a TDEE of 1,600–2,000 calories per day. A sedentary adult man typically 2,000–2,600. Active individuals can exceed 3,000–4,000 calories per day.
  • How accurate is the Mifflin-St Jeor formula?
    A 2005 meta-analysis found it predicted resting energy expenditure within 10% for 82% of non-obese adults. It is less accurate for very muscular individuals (underestimates) and those with high body fat (may overestimate). Treat the result as a starting estimate and adjust based on real-world results over 2–4 weeks.
  • Should I eat at my TDEE to maintain weight?
    Yes. Eating at your TDEE means your energy intake matches your energy expenditure, which maintains your current weight. Eating below TDEE creates a calorie deficit and leads to weight loss. Eating above TDEE creates a surplus and leads to weight gain.
  • How does activity level affect TDEE?
    Activity level has a large effect. A sedentary person multiplies their BMR by 1.2, while someone with a physically demanding job multiplies by 1.9 — a difference of nearly 60%. Choosing the wrong activity level is the most common source of inaccuracy. When in doubt, choose one level lower than you think.
